About Yalda Night

The Ancient Persian Celebration

Yalda Night (ÿ¥ÿ® €åŸÑÿØÿß) is one of the most important celebrations in Persian culture, marking the longest night of the year and the winter solstice. It falls on December 20-21 and celebrates the victory of light over darkness.

The word "Yalda" means "birth" in Aramaic, symbolizing the birth of the sun. This ancient celebration dates back over 7,000 years to the Zoroastrian era and continues to be a significant cultural and spiritual event for Persians worldwide.

Symbolism

Yalda represents the triumph of light over darkness, hope over despair, and good over evil. The red pomegranate symbolizes the sun, while watermelon represents the rebirth of spring.

Traditions

On Yalda night, families gather in homes decorated with candles and fruits. They share poems, stories, and food while staying awake until dawn to welcome the new sun. This night is considered auspicious for seeking guidance through divination.

Khwajeh Shams al-Din Muhammad Hafez (1315-1390)

Hafez was a Persian poet and mystic of the 14th century, considered one of the greatest poets in the Persian language. Born in Shiraz, Iran, Hafez was known for his lyrical poetry and spiritual wisdom that transcended religious and cultural boundaries.

His collection of poems, known as the "Divan of Hafez," contains approximately 500 lyrical poems called "Ghazals" (gazels). These poems explore themes of love, spirituality, mysticism, and the journey of the soul toward divine truth.

The Tradition of Hafez Faal (Oracle)

"Hafez Faal" is an ancient Persian tradition of seeking guidance through randomly selecting verses from Hafez's poetry collection. It is believed that the randomly chosen poem carries a message directly relevant to the seeker's current situation or questions.
